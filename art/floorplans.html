Floor Plans & Maps

Design the blueprint for your community's dream house, then zoom out and draw a full map of your entire town. Learn how architects and cartographers turn real places into drawings on paper — and create the official plans for the community you're building all week.

What Is a Floor Plan? What Is a Map?

Architects and cartographers (map-makers) both do the same thing: they take something huge in real life and draw it small enough to fit on a piece of paper. But they do it in different ways.

Floor Plan

A drawing of a building as if you sliced the roof off and looked straight down. It shows walls, rooms, doors, windows, and furniture — everything on one level. Architects use floor plans to design every house, school, and skyscraper before it gets built.

Map

A drawing of a larger area — a neighborhood, town, or whole country — seen from above. Maps show roads, buildings, rivers, parks, and other landmarks. Cartographers make maps so people can understand and navigate the world around them.

Why Do They Matter?

Nobody builds a house without a plan. Nobody explores a new place without a map. These drawings are how people communicate ideas about spaces — and your community needs both!

Understanding Scale

You can't draw a house at full size on a piece of paper — it would be enormous! That's where scale comes in. Scale means one small unit on your drawing represents a bigger unit in real life.

How Scale Works

On graph paper, you decide what each square represents. For a floor plan, 1 square = 1 foot works great. A bedroom that's 10 feet by 12 feet would be 10 squares by 12 squares on your paper. For a community map, you might use 1 square = 5 feet or 1 square = 10 feet to fit the whole town on one sheet.

Try It: Measure Your Desk

Use a ruler to measure your desk in inches. Now draw it on graph paper using the scale 1 square = 3 inches. If your desk is 24 inches wide, that's 8 squares on your paper. See? You just made a scale drawing!

Scale Bar

Every good floor plan and map includes a scale bar — a little ruler printed on the drawing that shows what distance on the paper equals in real life. Always include one!

Staying Consistent

Once you pick a scale, stick with it for the whole drawing. If 1 square = 1 foot for the kitchen, it has to be 1 square = 1 foot for the bedroom too. Otherwise nothing will be the right size!

Symbols & Legends

Architects and cartographers use special symbols so anyone can read their drawings — even without labels on everything.

Floor Plan Symbols

Thick lines = walls. A gap in a wall with a curved arc = a door (the arc shows which way it swings). A thin rectangle on a wall = a window. Small squares or circles inside rooms = furniture (beds, tables, sinks). Learn these symbols and your floor plan will look professional.

Map Symbols

On maps, you'll use different symbols: solid rectangles = buildings. Lines = roads (thicker lines for bigger roads). Green blobs = trees or parks. Blue lines = rivers or water. A star or special icon = important landmarks.

The Legend (Map Key)

A legend is a box on your map that explains what every symbol means. Draw each symbol next to its label so anyone picking up your map can understand it instantly. Every floor plan and map needs a legend!

Step 1 — Design Your Dream House Floor Plan

Every person in your community will design one house. This is the ideal home for the people who live in your town. Grab graph paper, a ruler, and colored pencils.

A. Plan Your Rooms

Before you draw, make a list of every room your house needs. Think about: bedrooms, bathrooms, kitchen, living room, dining room, garage, laundry room. How about special rooms — a game room? A library? A workshop? A rooftop garden? This is your dream house, so dream big (but it still has to fit on the paper!).

B. Set Your Scale

Use 1 square = 1 foot on your graph paper. A typical bedroom might be 10 x 12 squares. A bathroom might be 6 x 8 squares. A kitchen could be 12 x 14 squares. Sketch the outer walls first to set the total size of your house.

C. Draw the Walls

Using your ruler, draw thick straight lines for walls. Follow the grid lines exactly — no wobbly walls! Divide the interior into rooms. Make sure hallways are at least 3 squares wide (3 feet) so people can walk through.

D. Add Doors & Windows

Leave gaps in walls for doors and draw the swing arc. Add rectangles on exterior walls for windows. Think about which rooms need lots of light (living room, kitchen) and which need privacy (bathrooms).

E. Label & Color

Label every room with its name. Use colored pencils to color-code: blue for bathrooms, green for the kitchen, yellow for bedrooms, orange for living spaces. Add a scale bar and a legend. Write the name of your house at the top!

Step 2 — Create Your Community Map

Now zoom out. Your whole community of 6 works together to draw a map of the entire town — showing every building, road, park, and landmark.

A. Plan the Layout

As a team, decide where everything goes. Where are the houses? The school? The store? The park? The bridge from engineering class? Sketch a rough layout on scrap paper first before committing to the final version.

B. Choose a Bigger Scale

For the community map, use 1 square = 5 feet or 1 square = 10 feet. This lets you fit the whole town on one large sheet of graph paper. Your houses will be small rectangles now, not full floor plans.

C. Draw Roads First

Roads are the skeleton of your town. Draw main roads first with thick lines, then add side streets. Use your ruler! Name your roads — Main Street, Science Drive, Engineer's Way, whatever fits your community.

D. Add Buildings & Landmarks

Draw buildings as rectangles along the roads. Color them in and label them. Add parks (color green), ponds or rivers (color blue), trees (small circles), and any other features. Don't forget the bridge, the tower, and other structures from your STEAM projects!

E. Add Legend, Scale Bar & Compass Rose

Include a legend explaining all your symbols, a scale bar showing distance, and a compass rose showing North, South, East, and West. Use colored markers for clean, bold labels.

Tools & Techniques

Rulers Are Your Best Friend

Straight lines make the difference between a professional-looking plan and a messy sketch. Always use a ruler for walls, roads, and edges. Take your time — architects don't rush.

Colored Pencils for Detail

Use colored pencils for shading rooms, coloring parks, and adding texture. Press lightly for large areas and harder for outlines. Layer colors for richer effects.

Markers for Labels

Use colored markers for titles, room names, road names, and legend text. Markers are bold and easy to read from a distance — important for the exposition!

Pencil First, Then Ink

Always sketch in light pencil first. Once you're happy with the layout, go over it in pen or marker. Erase the pencil lines for a clean final product.

Step 3 — Assemble Your Tri-Fold Board

Your floor plans and community map will be displayed on a tri-fold presentation board at the exposition. This is how real architects present their work to clients!

Layout Your Board

The left panel can show individual house floor plans. The center panel (the biggest) features the full community map. The right panel can include your legend, written descriptions of key buildings, and any close-up detail drawings.

Add Written Descriptions

For each important building on your map, write a short description: What is it? Who uses it? Why did your community need it? This connects your art to the writing skills you're learning.

Make It Presentable

Add a big title at the top of the center panel: your community's name and "Town Plan." Use neat lettering and borders. Mount your drawings on colored construction paper before gluing to the board for a polished look.

Your Blueprints, Your Community

This isn't just a drawing exercise — these are the actual plans for the community you're building all week at camp. The houses from your floor plans will match the structures from engineering. The roads on your map will connect the bridge, the tower, and every other project.

Everything Connects

The buildings on your map should match what you're constructing in engineering class. The technology projects (cars, solar panels) need roads and space on the map. The science experiments need a lab building. Your community map is the master plan that ties every STEAM subject together.

Challenge Levels

Basic

Complete a floor plan of your dream house with all rooms labeled, walls drawn with a ruler, doors and windows marked, and a scale bar included.

Medium

Complete both a house floor plan AND a community map with a legend, scale bar, compass rose, named roads, and all buildings labeled. Both drawings use consistent scale.

Advanced

Create a full tri-fold presentation board featuring multiple floor plans, a detailed community map, written descriptions of each building's purpose, and a scale that's mathematically accurate. Be ready to present and explain your design choices at the exposition.
