Reports & Communication

Scientists write research papers. Engineers write technical reports. Businesses write ads and brochures. No matter what you build or discover, you need to communicate it clearly. Learn to write reports, design brochures, compose professional letters, and present your ideas with confidence.

Why Communication Matters in STEAM

You might think STEAM is all about building and experimenting. But here's the truth: if you can't explain what you did and why it matters, it's like it never happened.

Scientists Write Papers

When scientists discover something, they write a research paper explaining their experiment, data, and conclusions. Other scientists read it, check the work, and build on it. No paper = no one knows about the discovery.

Engineers Write Reports

Before a bridge gets built, engineers write reports explaining why their design is safe. After testing, they write reports on what worked and what didn't. Reports turn ideas into trust.

Businesses Write Ads

Every brochure, website, and commercial is the work of a writer. The words have to be clear, persuasive, and exciting. A great product with bad advertising goes nowhere.

Leaders Give Presentations

Presidents, CEOs, teachers, and coaches all need to speak clearly and confidently. At the exposition, YOU are the leader presenting your community's work. Strong communication makes people listen.

Skill 1 — Report Writing

A report is a structured piece of writing that explains what you did, what happened, and what it means. Every STEAM project you've worked on this week deserves a report.

The Structure of a Report

Every good report has the same basic parts:

Title: Clear and specific. Not "My Report" — more like "Spaghetti Bridge Strength Test Results."
Introduction: What were you trying to do? What was the goal of the project or experiment?
Process: What did you do, step by step? Write this so someone who wasn't there could understand exactly what happened.
Findings: What happened? What did you observe, measure, or discover?
Conclusion: What does it mean? What did you learn? What would you do differently next time?

Tips for Strong Report Writing

Use complete sentences — no fragments or bullet points in your final version. Write in the past tense ("We tested..." not "We test..."). Be specific — say "the bridge held 47 pennies" not "the bridge held a lot." Check your grammar and spelling — professionals proofread their work before publishing.

Activity: Write a STEAM Report

Pick one project you've worked on at camp — the spaghetti bridge, the solar car, the Sierpinski pyramid, a science experiment, anything. Write a one-page report following the structure above. Include a title, introduction, process, findings, and conclusion. This report will go on your community's tri-fold display.

Skill 2 — Brochure Design

A brochure is a folded piece of paper that advertises something — a town, a business, an event, a product. Your job: create a brochure that makes people want to visit your community's town.

How to Fold a Brochure

Take a regular piece of paper and fold it into thirds (like folding a letter to put in an envelope). Now you have 6 panels: 3 on the front side and 3 on the back. The front panel (the right third when folded) is your cover. The back panel (the left third of the back side) has contact info or a map. The inside panels tell the full story.

What Goes on a Brochure

Cover: An eye-catching headline and an image. "Welcome to [Your Town Name]!" or "Discover [Town Name] — Where STEAM Comes Alive!"
Inside panels: What makes your town special? Describe key attractions — the solar-powered houses, the amazing bridge, the beautiful park, the science lab. Use subheadings to organize: "Things to See," "Things to Do," "Why Live Here?"
Back panel: A small map of your town (from your floor plans project!), the community's name, and a catchy closing tagline.

Persuasive Writing

A brochure doesn't just inform — it persuades. You want the reader to think, "I need to visit this town!" Use:

Power words: amazing, stunning, state-of-the-art, innovative, beautiful, exciting, one-of-a-kind.
Benefits, not just features: Don't just say "We have a park." Say "Relax in our beautiful community park, featuring shade trees and a walking trail."
Call to action: End with something that tells the reader what to do next: "Visit us at the STEAM Exposition!" or "Come see it for yourself!"

Design Tip: White Space

Don't cram every inch with text. Leave space between sections. This makes your brochure easier to read and more professional-looking.

Design Tip: Headlines First

Write your headlines bigger and bolder than body text. People scan brochures quickly — headlines catch their eye and pull them in to read more.

Design Tip: Add Color & Pictures

Draw small illustrations of your town's attractions. Use colored markers for headlines and colored pencils for drawings. A colorful brochure beats a plain one every time.

Skill 3 — Professional Correspondence

A professional letter or email is how you communicate with people in a formal, respectful way. Whether you're writing to a principal, a business owner, or a guest of honor, the format matters.

Parts of a Formal Letter

Date: Always include the date at the top.
Greeting: "Dear Mr./Ms./Dr. [Last Name]," — use their proper title. If you don't know the name, "Dear Sir or Madam," works.
Body paragraph 1: Introduce yourself and state your purpose. "My name is [Name], and I am a student at [Camp Name]. I am writing to invite you to..."
Body paragraph 2: Give details. When is it? Where is it? What will they see?
Body paragraph 3: Express appreciation. "We would be honored to have you attend."
Closing: "Sincerely," or "Respectfully," followed by your signature and printed name.

Tone and Grammar

Professional writing has a different tone than how you text your friends. No slang. No abbreviations. No emojis. Complete sentences, proper punctuation, and respectful language. Read your letter out loud — if it sounds like something an adult professional would write, you're on track.

Activity: Write an Invitation Letter

Write a formal letter inviting a special guest (a teacher, principal, community leader, or family member) to visit your community's exposition. Follow the format above. Include the date, time, and location of the expo, what the guest will see, and why their visit would be meaningful. Proofread carefully — first impressions matter!

Skill 4 — Presentation Skills

At the exposition, you won't just show your work — you'll talk about it. Presenting is a skill that takes practice, but these tips will help you shine.

Know Your Material

Don't read from a script word for word. Know your project well enough to explain it in your own words. Practice by explaining it to someone who knows nothing about it.

Eye Contact

Look at the people you're talking to, not at the floor or your poster. Make eye contact with different people as you speak. This makes you look confident and keeps your audience engaged.

Speak Clearly & Loudly

Project your voice so everyone can hear. Speak at a steady pace — not too fast, not too slow. Pronounce your words clearly. If you're nervous, take a breath and slow down.

Use Your Board

Point to your tri-fold board as you talk. "Here you can see our bridge design..." Visuals help your audience follow along and give you something to do with your hands.

Activity: Practice Presentation

With your community, practice presenting one of your STEAM projects. Each person should explain one part. Time yourselves — aim for 2-3 minutes total. Give each other feedback: Was it clear? Loud enough? Did the speaker make eye contact? Practice at least twice before the real exposition.

Organizing Your Tri-Fold Board

Your tri-fold board is the visual anchor for your presentation. A well-organized board makes your whole team look professional.

Board Layout

Top center: Your community's name in big, bold letters.
Left panel: Reports and written findings from your STEAM projects.
Center panel: Key visuals — photos, drawings, diagrams, and charts from your experiments and builds.
Right panel: Your brochure, invitation letter, and any other communication pieces.

Design Principles

Use neat handwriting or printed text. Mount papers on colored construction paper for borders. Keep spacing even. Use a consistent color scheme that matches your community's theme. Titles should be readable from 3 feet away. Body text should be readable from 2 feet away.

Everything Connects

Your Writing Tells Your Community's Story

Your reports document what you built in engineering and technology. Your brochure advertises the town you designed in art. Your letter invites guests to see your science experiments. Your presentation ties it all together. Communication is the thread that connects every STEAM subject into one complete community story.

Challenge Levels

Basic

Write a one-page report on a STEAM project (with title, introduction, process, findings, and conclusion) AND create a simple tri-fold brochure advertising your community's town with headlines, descriptions, and at least one illustration.

Medium

Complete a report and brochure, plus write a professional invitation letter following the formal format. Design a polished brochure with persuasive writing, subheadings, graphics, and a call to action.

Advanced

Create a full communication portfolio: a detailed report, a professional brochure with persuasive copy, a formal invitation letter, AND deliver a practiced 2-3 minute presentation to your community. All pieces should be proofread, polished, and ready for the exposition tri-fold board.
