Sierpinski Pyramid

Build a giant Sierpinski tetrahedron — a fractal pyramid made of smaller pyramids, made of even smaller pyramids. Your entire community of 6 works together to color, fold, and assemble this mind-bending mathematical sculpture.

What Is a Fractal?

A fractal is a shape that looks the same no matter how much you zoom in. The same pattern repeats at every level — big, medium, small, tiny. They're everywhere in nature.

Broccoli

Break off a piece of broccoli. It looks like... a tiny broccoli. Break off a piece of that — same shape again!

Snowflakes

The branching patterns of a snowflake repeat at every scale. Each arm has smaller arms that look just like the big one.

Trees

A tree trunk splits into branches. Each branch splits into smaller branches. Each of those splits again. Same pattern, different size.

Coastlines

Zoom into a map of a coastline — the wiggly edge looks the same whether you're looking at a whole country or a single bay.

Meet the Sierpinski Triangle

The Sierpinski triangle is one of the most famous fractals. Here's how it works:

The Rule

Start with a triangle. Connect the midpoints of each side to make a smaller triangle in the center. Remove that center triangle. Now you have 3 smaller triangles. Repeat the rule on each of those 3 triangles. Then repeat again. And again. Forever.

The Math Behind It

Each step triples the number of triangles. Step 0: 1 triangle. Step 1: 3. Step 2: 9. Step 3: 27. That's 3 × 3 × 3 — or 3 to the power of the step number. This is called exponential growth, and it's why fractals get incredibly complex incredibly fast.

From 2D to 3D

The Sierpinski triangle is flat (2D). But what if we build it in 3D? Instead of triangles, we use tetrahedrons — pyramids with 4 triangular faces. A Sierpinski tetrahedron (or "Sierpinski pyramid") is the same fractal pattern, but as a real 3D object you can hold in your hands.

The Surprise Connection: Pascal's Triangle

Here's something wild — the Sierpinski triangle is hiding inside one of the most famous number patterns in all of math: Pascal's Triangle.

What Is Pascal's Triangle?

Pascal's Triangle is a number pyramid where each number is the sum of the two numbers directly above it. Start with a 1 at the top. Each row starts and ends with 1, and every other number is made by adding the two above it:

1
1   1
1   2   1
1   3   3   1
1   4   6   4   1
1   5   10   10   5   1
1   6   15   20   15   6   1
1   7   21   35   35   21   7   1

See how it works? Row 4 is 1, 4, 6, 4, 1 because: 1, (1+3), (3+3), (3+1), 1. Each number is the sum of the two above it.

The Hidden Sierpinski Pattern

Now here's the magic. Take Pascal's Triangle and color in only the ODD numbers. Leave the even numbers blank. Draw at least 8 rows (more is better — try 16 if you can!).

When you shade all the odd numbers and step back... the Sierpinski Triangle appears! The pattern of odd numbers forms the exact same fractal shape — triangles inside triangles inside triangles. This is a surprising and beautiful connection between algebra (number patterns) and geometry (triangle shapes).

Try It Yourself!

Grab a sheet of paper and draw Pascal's Triangle with at least 8 rows (16 is even better). Then shade every odd number with a colored pencil and leave even numbers white. Watch the Sierpinski pattern emerge right before your eyes. The more rows you draw, the more clearly you'll see the fractal!

The Build Plan

Your community of 6 will build a Stage 3 Sierpinski pyramid. That means 64 individual tetrahedrons assembled into one giant structure. Here's how the stages work:

Stage 0: One Tetrahedron

A single paper pyramid. 4 triangular faces. This is the building block — every person makes several of these.

Stage 1: 4 Tetrahedrons

Glue 4 Stage 0 pyramids together — one on each bottom corner and one on top. The center stays empty. This is where the fractal pattern begins!

Stage 2: 16 Tetrahedrons

Take 4 Stage 1 clusters and arrange them the same way — three on the bottom corners, one on top. Same pattern, bigger scale.

Stage 3: 64 Tetrahedrons

Four Stage 2 clusters assembled in the same pattern. This is your community's final pyramid — it'll be big!

Step 1 — Make the Tetrahedrons

Each person in your community needs to make about 11 tetrahedrons (64 total ÷ 6 people = about 11 each). You'll be using card stock for this build — it's thicker and sturdier than regular paper, so your pyramids will hold their shape and support the weight of the bigger structure. Here's how.

A. Cut the Template

Each tetrahedron is made from a 2-dimensional net — that's the flat pattern that folds up into a 3D shape. The net is a strip of 4 equilateral triangles connected in a row with a tab for gluing. You'll cut these nets from card stock (not regular paper!) because card stock is sturdier and holds its shape much better when folded into a pyramid. Your teacher will provide the template. Trace it onto card stock, then cut it out carefully.

B. Color Your Triangles

This is where the art comes in! Each community should pick a color scheme. Some ideas: each person uses a different color, gradient from light to dark, rainbow pattern, or your community's team colors. Color both sides of the paper so it looks good from every angle.

C. Fold and Glue

Score along all fold lines using a ruler and the back of scissors (press firmly but don't cut through). Fold each triangle up. Tuck the tab inside and glue it shut. You now have a perfect tetrahedron!

Quality Check

Make sure each tetrahedron has clean edges, all faces are flat (not dented), and it sits flat on any face without wobbling. Bad tetrahedrons will make assembly harder — take your time!

Step 2 — Assemble Stage 1 Clusters

Now pairs work together to combine 4 tetrahedrons into Stage 1 clusters.

How to Arrange

Place 3 tetrahedrons flat on the table in a triangle formation, with edges touching. Glue the touching edges together. Now place the 4th tetrahedron on top, sitting in the valley between the 3 bottom ones. Glue it in place. You now have a Stage 1 cluster — a mini Sierpinski pyramid!

Notice the Pattern

Look at your Stage 1 from the side. See the triangle shape? And in the center, there's an empty space shaped like an upside-down tetrahedron. That empty space IS the fractal — it's the piece you removed, just like the Sierpinski triangle rule.

How Many Stage 1 Clusters?

Your team needs 16 Stage 1 clusters (64 ÷ 4 = 16). With 6 people, that's about 2-3 clusters each. Assembly line it!

Step 3 — Assemble Stage 2

Same pattern, bigger scale. Take 4 Stage 1 clusters and arrange them the same way.

How to Arrange

Place 3 Stage 1 clusters on the table in a triangle formation with edges touching. Glue them together. Place the 4th on top. Glue carefully — it's heavier now, so hold it steady while the glue sets.

You Need 4 Stage 2 Clusters

16 Stage 1 clusters ÷ 4 = 4 Stage 2 clusters. Split your team: two pairs each build 2 Stage 2 clusters.

Step 4 — Final Assembly: Stage 3

The whole community comes together for this one.

The Grand Build

Same pattern one last time. Three Stage 2 clusters on the bottom in a triangle. The 4th on top. Glue everything carefully. Support the top piece while it dries — this is a team effort!

Step Back and Look

You've built a fractal. Every part of it is a smaller version of the whole thing. Stage 3 is made of 4 Stage 2s. Each Stage 2 is made of 4 Stage 1s. Each Stage 1 is made of 4 tetrahedrons. Same pattern, four levels deep.

The Math Behind Your Pyramid

Your pyramid is a math lesson you can hold.

Powers of 4

Stage 0: 4⁰ = 1. Stage 1: 4¹ = 4. Stage 2: 4² = 16. Stage 3: 4³ = 64. Each stage multiplies by 4.

Self-Similarity

Zoom into any corner of your pyramid — it looks exactly like the whole thing, just smaller. That's the defining property of a fractal.

Fractal Dimension

A line is 1D. A square is 2D. A cube is 3D. The Sierpinski pyramid has a fractal dimension of about 2 — it lives in 3D space but has holes everywhere, so it's not quite a solid. Mind-bending!

Surface Area vs. Volume

As you add more stages, the surface area grows but the volume keeps getting holes punched out of it. At infinite stages, the Sierpinski tetrahedron has infinite surface area but zero volume.

Challenge Levels

Basic

Make all 64 tetrahedrons cleanly and assemble the full Stage 3 pyramid. It stands on its own and looks great.

Medium

Use a deliberate color pattern that shows the fractal levels — like different colors for each stage. Can someone looking at it tell where Stage 1 ends and Stage 2 begins?

Advanced

Calculate the total surface area of your pyramid (measure one tetrahedron, multiply). Figure out what fraction of the total volume is empty space. Present the math to your community.

Present Your Pyramid

At the expo, your Sierpinski pyramid will be the centerpiece of your community display. Be ready to explain:

What to Present

What is a fractal? How does the Sierpinski pattern work? How many tetrahedrons are in your pyramid? What does 4³ mean and why? What is self-similarity — can you show it by pointing to different parts of your pyramid? How did your team divide up the work?
