Logic & Critical Thinking

Think like a detective, scientist, and programmer all at once. You'll tackle classic puzzles, explore mind-bending paradoxes, and learn strategies for cracking any problem — then use those skills to help plan your community.

What Is Logic?

Logic is the art of thinking clearly and carefully. It's how we figure out what's true, what's false, and what follows from what. Every time you solve a mystery, debug a computer program, design a bridge, or settle an argument fairly, you're using logic.

Detectives

A detective finds clues and uses logic to figure out what happened. "If the window was locked from the inside, the thief must have used the door." That's logical reasoning!

Scientists

Scientists form hypotheses and test them. If the experiment doesn't match the prediction, the hypothesis must be wrong. Logic helps them narrow down the truth.

Programmers

Every line of code is logic: IF this happens, THEN do that, ELSE do something different. Computers follow logic perfectly — they do exactly what you tell them, nothing more.

Everyday Life

Should you bring an umbrella? If the sky is cloudy AND the forecast says rain, THEN yes. You use logic dozens of times a day without even thinking about it.

Types of Logical Thinking

There are two main ways we reason through problems. Both are powerful, and great thinkers use them together.

Deductive Reasoning: From General to Specific

Deductive reasoning starts with a general rule and applies it to a specific case. If the rule is true, and the case fits the rule, then the conclusion must be true.

Example: All dogs are animals. Fido is a dog. Therefore, Fido is an animal. There's no way around it — if those first two statements are true, the conclusion is guaranteed.

Try it: All squares have 4 equal sides. This shape has 4 equal sides and 4 right angles. Is it a square? (Yes!)

Inductive Reasoning: From Specific to General

Inductive reasoning looks at specific examples and spots a pattern. The conclusion is probably true, but not guaranteed — you're making an educated guess based on evidence.

Example: Every swan you've ever seen is white. You conclude: "All swans are white." That seems reasonable — until someone finds a black swan in Australia! Inductive reasoning can be wrong, so we always keep testing.

Try it: You notice that 2, 4, 6, 8 are all even. You conclude: "All even numbers are divisible by 2." Is that always true? (Yes — that one happens to be correct!)

Classic Logic Puzzles

These famous puzzles have been challenging thinkers for centuries. Grab some paper and a pencil and see if your team can crack them!

The Wolf, Goat, and Cabbage

A farmer needs to cross a river with a wolf, a goat, and a cabbage. The boat can only carry the farmer plus one item at a time. But if left alone together: the wolf will eat the goat, and the goat will eat the cabbage. How does the farmer get everything across safely?

Hint: Think about what can safely be left together. The wolf won't eat the cabbage! The trick is that sometimes you need to bring something back across the river.

Solution: Take the goat across. Come back. Take the wolf across. Bring the goat back. Take the cabbage across. Come back. Take the goat across. Done in 7 trips!

Logic Grid Puzzles: Who Lives Where?

Three friends — Alex, Blake, and Casey — live in three different colored houses: red, blue, and green. Use these clues to figure out who lives where:

  • Alex does not live in the red house.
  • Blake does not live in the blue house.
  • The person in the green house is not Casey.

Strategy: Make a grid with names on one side and house colors on the other. Put an X where something is impossible. When only one option is left in a row or column, that must be the answer!

Solution: Alex lives in the blue house, Blake lives in the green house, and Casey lives in the red house.

The Monty Hall Problem

You're on a game show. There are 3 doors. Behind one door is a prize. Behind the other two: nothing. You pick Door 1. The host (who knows what's behind every door) opens Door 3, showing nothing. The host asks: "Do you want to switch to Door 2?"

Should you switch? Most people say it doesn't matter — it's 50/50, right? WRONG! You should always switch. Here's why: when you first picked, you had a 1-in-3 chance of being right. That means there was a 2-in-3 chance the prize was behind one of the other doors. When the host opens an empty door, all of that 2-in-3 chance goes to the remaining door. Switching wins 2 out of 3 times!

Try it: Test this with a friend using 3 cups and a small object. Play 30 rounds — sometimes switch, sometimes don't. Count your wins. The math really works!

Pattern Recognition Puzzles

What comes next in each sequence?

  • 2, 6, 18, 54, ___ (Each number is multiplied by 3. Answer: 162)
  • 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, ___ (Each number is the sum of the two before it — the Fibonacci sequence! Answer: 21)
  • O, T, T, F, F, S, S, ___ (First letters of One, Two, Three, Four, Five, Six, Seven... Answer: E for Eight)

Pattern recognition is one of the most important thinking skills. Scientists use it to find laws of nature. Programmers use it to spot bugs. You use it every time you predict what will happen next.

Paradoxes: Mind-Bending Ideas

A paradox is a statement or situation that seems to contradict itself or doesn't make logical sense — even though it sounds like it should. Paradoxes push our brains to their limits and show us where language and logic get weird.

The Liar Paradox

Consider this sentence: "This statement is false."

If the statement IS true, then what it says must be correct — so it's false. But if it's false, then it's NOT false — so it must be true. But if it's true... we're going in circles! There is no answer. This is called a diametric opposition — the statement pulls in two opposite directions at the same time.

The Ship of Theseus

Imagine a famous old ship. Over the years, every plank of wood, every nail, every rope is replaced one at a time. After all parts have been replaced, is it still the same ship? And if someone collected all the old parts and built a ship from them — which one is the "real" original ship?

Discuss with your team: If you replace every cell in your body over 7 years, are you the same person? What about a sports team where all the players change — is it still the same team?

The Crocodile Dilemma

A crocodile steals a child and tells the parent: "I'll return your child if you can correctly predict what I'll do." The parent says: "You will NOT return my child." Now the crocodile is stuck. If it returns the child, the parent was wrong — so it shouldn't have returned the child. If it keeps the child, the parent was right — so it should return the child!

How to Approach Any Problem

Great logical thinkers have strategies. Here are tools you can use to crack any problem, whether it's a brain teaser, a math question, or a real-world challenge.

Break It Down

Big problems are made of smaller problems. Instead of panicking, ask: "What's the first small thing I need to figure out?" Solve the pieces, then put the answer together.

Eliminate Impossibilities

Like Sherlock Holmes said: "When you eliminate the impossible, whatever remains — however unlikely — must be the truth." Cross off what CAN'T be true and see what's left.

Work Backwards

Start from the answer and trace back. If you know where you need to end up, the path to get there is often much clearer than starting from the beginning.

Look for Patterns

Try a few examples and see if a pattern emerges. Patterns are shortcuts — once you spot one, you can predict answers without working through every single case.

Logic and Your Community

Here's where it gets real. Your community needs to be planned logically. Every building, road, and service needs to make sense. Use what you've learned to think through these decisions.

Community Planning Challenges

Work with your team to answer these questions using logical reasoning:

  • Where should the hospital go? It needs to be accessible from everywhere in town. Use deductive reasoning: emergency vehicles need fast routes, so the hospital should be near a main road and centrally located.
  • What's the most logical road layout? A grid is efficient for navigation but might not fit the landscape. A spoke-and-hub pattern puts everything close to the center. What are the pros and cons of each?
  • Where do you put the school vs. the factory? Think about noise, traffic, and safety. What are the logical constraints? (The factory shouldn't be next to the school or homes. The school should be in a residential area.)
  • How many fire stations does your community need? If a fire truck needs to reach any building within 5 minutes, and it travels a certain speed, what's the maximum distance? Use that to figure out how many stations you need and where they go.

Hands-On: Team Puzzle Challenge

Time to put your skills to the test! Your team will work through puzzles together using paper, pencils, and hands-on materials. Talk through your reasoning out loud — explaining your thinking is just as important as getting the right answer.

Round 1: River Crossing Variations

Now that you know the wolf, goat, and cabbage solution, try a harder version: Three missionaries and three cannibals need to cross a river. The boat holds 2 people. If cannibals ever outnumber missionaries on either bank, it's game over. Can you get everyone across? Use coins or game pieces to act it out.

Round 2: Logic Grid Challenge

Your teacher will give each team a logic grid puzzle with 4 or 5 categories. Use the grid, mark your X's and check marks, and work through the clues one at a time. Remember: eliminate the impossible!

Round 3: Community Layout

Using a large sheet of paper, sketch a logical layout for your community. Where do buildings go? Where are the roads? Explain why each choice makes logical sense. Be ready to defend your decisions to another team!

Challenge Levels

Basic

Solve 3 guided puzzles (river crossing, logic grid, and one pattern puzzle). Write down your solution steps for each one so someone else could follow your thinking.

Medium

Solve all 3 guided puzzles plus 2 harder bonus puzzles. For each one, write a clear explanation of your reasoning — what strategies did you use? Where did you get stuck and how did you get unstuck?

Advanced

Create your own original logic puzzle for another team to solve. It must have exactly one solution, include at least 4 clues, and come with a written solution key. Test it to make sure it works before handing it off!
