Notecard Buildings

Construct buildings for your community using only notecards, scissors, and glue. Design schools, shops, restaurants, and landmarks that bring your town to life. This is where engineering meets creativity.

What Does Your Town Need?

Your community already has smart houses, robot cars, a bridge, and a tower. Now it's time to fill in the rest of your town. What buildings does every community need?

School

Where kids learn! Could have a playground, classrooms, and a flag on top.

Grocery Store

Keeps the town fed. Big front windows to show off the produce inside.

Library

A place for knowledge and community. Maybe a two-story design with big doors?

Restaurant

Every town needs a place to eat. Add outdoor seating or a drive-through for your robot cars!

Park / Playground

Open space with benches, trees, and a place for people to gather.

Your Idea!

Movie theater? Skate park? Pet shop? Your community is unique — build what it needs.

Step 1 — Notecard Engineering Basics

A flat notecard is floppy and weak. But with a few folds, it becomes a strong building material.

The L-Fold

Fold a notecard in half lengthwise to make an L-shape. Stand it up — it's now a wall that doesn't fall over. Two L-folds facing each other make a corner or a doorway.

The Box Method

Score and fold a single notecard into a rectangular box shape. Tape or glue the edges. This creates a solid 3D structure that can support weight on top — perfect for stacking stories.

The Accordion

Fold a notecard back and forth like a fan. Stand it up — it's now a strong, wide wall. Accordions are great for supporting roofs or making decorative facades.

Tabs and Slots

Cut small tabs at the edge of a card and small slots in another. Slide the tab into the slot — no glue needed! This is how real cardboard furniture and displays are assembled.

Step 2 — Plan Your Building

Every building starts with a blueprint. Sketch yours before you cut.

The Rules

You can use notecards (index cards), scissors, glue (or tape), and markers/colored pencils for decoration. Each pair builds at least one building for the community. It must be freestanding and have at least one door or opening.

Draw All Four Sides

Sketch the front, back, and both sides of your building. Mark where doors and windows go. Think about: How tall is it? Does it have a flat roof or a pitched (triangle) roof? Are there any special features like a sign, awning, or balcony?

Tip: Scale

Think about how your building fits next to the smart houses and robot cars. If the car is 6 inches long, a building shouldn't be 2 inches tall!

Tip: Reinforce

Double up cards on walls that need to hold up a roof. A single card wall is okay for short buildings, but two-story designs need thicker walls.

Step 3 — Build Your Building

Start with the walls, then add the roof, then details.

A. Build the Walls

Cut notecards to size for each wall. Score fold lines where walls meet at corners. Use L-folds or tabs and slots to connect walls together. Make sure the base is square — if walls aren't at right angles, the roof won't sit flat.

B. Add the Roof

For a flat roof: lay a card across the top of the walls and glue it down. For a pitched roof: fold a card in half and set it on top like a tent. For a fancy roof: cut triangle gable ends and glue the folded roof card to them.

C. Cut Doors and Windows

Use scissors to cut openings. Pro tip: cut a door on three sides and fold it open — now you have a door that actually opens! For windows, cut a square and fold the flaps outward to make shutters.

D. Decorate

Use markers or colored pencils to add bricks, signs, window frames, and personality. Draw what's inside visible through the windows. Add a name sign above the door. Make it look like a place people would want to visit!

Step 4 — Challenge Levels

Basic

Build a single-story building with 4 walls, a roof, and at least one door. It stands on its own and has a name sign.

Medium

Build a two-story building or an L-shaped building. Add windows that open, interior walls, and detailed decoration.

Advanced

Build a complex structure — a multi-story building, a building with a unique roof shape, or a building with moving parts (opening doors, a garage for robot cars). Add landscaping like fences or trees.

Step 5 — Place It in Your Town

When your building is done, work with your community team to decide where it goes in town.

Town Planning Questions

Is this building near the road so robot cars can reach it? Does it need to be near other buildings (a school near houses, a store near the town center)? How does it connect to the bridge and tower? Does your economic system include this business?

Present Your Building

Explain to your community: What is this building? Who uses it? How did you construct it? What engineering techniques did you use (L-folds, tabs, box method)? What would you add with more time?
