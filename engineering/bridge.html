Spaghetti Bridge

Design and build a bridge using only spaghetti and hot glue. Your bridge needs to span a gap and hold as much weight as possible. Learn about trusses, tension, compression, and why triangles are an engineer's best friend.

Your Bridge, Your Community

Every community needs bridges — to connect neighborhoods, cross rivers, and link people together. Your team's bridge will connect two parts of your town. Before you build, decide:

What does it connect?

Two neighborhoods? A house to a school? The town to a park across a river? Give your bridge a purpose.

What crosses it?

People walking? Cars driving? Robot cars from your tech project? This affects how strong it needs to be.

Name your bridge!

Real bridges have names. What will yours be called? Make it meaningful to your community.

Step 1 — How Do Bridges Work?

Before building, you need to understand the forces that every bridge fights against.

Two Key Forces

Compression is a pushing force — it squishes things together. When you stand on a bridge, your weight pushes down. Tension is a pulling force — it stretches things apart. The bottom of a bridge gets pulled as the top gets pushed. A good bridge handles both.

Why Triangles?

Try pushing on the corner of a square — it collapses into a diamond. Now imagine pushing on a triangle — it doesn't budge. Triangles are the strongest shape in engineering because they distribute force evenly along all three sides. This is why bridges, towers, and buildings use triangular trusses.

What Is a Truss?

A truss is a structure made of triangles connected together. Real bridges like highway overpasses use trusses. Your spaghetti bridge should too!

Step 2 — Plan Your Design

Real engineers don't just start building — they sketch first. Grab a pencil and paper.

The Rules

Your bridge must span at least 10 inches (the gap between two tables or books). You can only use spaghetti (uncooked!) and hot glue. The bridge that holds the most weight wins.

Sketch It Out

Draw your bridge from the side. Mark where the triangles will go. Think about: How wide is the base? How tall is the truss? Where will you put the road surface (the flat part things cross on)?

Design Tip: Double Up

A single strand of spaghetti breaks easily. But 2-3 strands glued together are much stronger. Bundle your spaghetti for load-bearing parts.

Design Tip: Wide Base

A wider base = more stability. If your bridge is too narrow, it'll tip sideways when weight is added.

Design Tip: Symmetry

Build both sides the same. If one side is stronger than the other, the bridge will twist and fail at the weak point.

Step 3 — Build Your Bridge

Time to build! Work with your partner and take it step by step.

A. Build the Side Panels

Lay spaghetti flat on wax paper. Build two identical side panels using triangles. Glue each joint carefully and let it dry before moving. These are your trusses — the skeleton of the bridge.

B. Add the Road Deck

Lay spaghetti flat across the bottom to create a flat surface. This is where the weight will sit. Glue the deck to both side panels.

C. Add Cross Bracing

Connect the two side panels with cross pieces. This stops the bridge from wobbling sideways. Add diagonal braces between the panels for extra strength.

D. Let It Dry

Seriously — let the glue fully dry. Rushing this step is the #1 reason bridges fail. Use this time to decorate or plan your community presentation.

Step 4 — Test It!

The moment of truth. Set your bridge across the gap and start adding weight.

How to Test

Place your bridge between two supports (books or table edges). Hang a small cup or container from the center of the bridge using string. Slowly add weight — coins, washers, or small objects — one at a time. Record how much weight it holds before breaking.

Challenge Levels

Basic

Build a bridge that spans the gap without collapsing under its own weight. It stands up — that's a win!

Medium

Build a bridge that holds at least 1 pound (about 100 pennies or a small water bottle).

Advanced

Build a bridge that holds 2+ pounds. Analyze where it failed — was it compression on top? Tension on the bottom? Redesign and try again.

Step 5 — Engineer's Report

Real engineers write reports after every project. Answer these questions with your partner:

Reflection Questions

What shape did you use most in your design, and why? Where did your bridge break first — what does that tell you about the forces? If you could rebuild it, what would you change? How does your bridge help your community?
