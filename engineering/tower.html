Step 1 — What Makes a Tower Stand?

Before you tape a single straw, understand why some towers stand and others don't.

The Three Enemies of Towers

Gravity pulls everything down. The taller your tower, the more weight the bottom has to hold up. Buckling happens when a straw bends under weight — straws are strong in tension (pulling) but weak in compression (pushing). Wobble is side-to-side movement. Wind, bumps, or an uneven base all cause wobble.

How to Fight Back

Wide base — a pyramid shape spreads weight out and resists tipping. Triangles — add diagonal braces to turn wobbly squares into rigid triangles. Short segments — a short straw buckles less than a long one. Use small sections connected together.

Step 2 — Plan Your Design

Grab pencil and paper. Sketch your tower from the front and from the top (bird's eye view).

The Rules

You can only use straws and tape (masking tape or painter's tape). The tower must be freestanding — no taping it to the table! Tallest tower that stands on its own for 10 seconds wins.

Strategy: Pyramid

Start wide at the base and get narrower as you go up. Very stable, but uses lots of straws on the bottom.

Strategy: Square Tube

Build a square cross-section and go straight up. Add diagonal braces on every face to prevent wobble.

Strategy: Triangle Tube

Three-sided tower. Uses fewer straws per level than a square, and every face is already a triangle. Very efficient.

Step 3 — Build Your Tower

Work from the bottom up. The base is the most important part — take your time.

A. Build the Base

Make your base shape first (square, triangle, or wider polygon). Tape all corners firmly. Add diagonal braces so the base doesn't wobble at all. Test it — press down on it. If it flexes, add more braces.

B. Build Up in Sections

Add one level at a time. Each level should be a copy of your base shape, connected by vertical straws. After each level, add diagonal braces on every face. Check if it's leaning — fix it before adding more height.

C. Taper the Top

As you get higher, you can make each level slightly smaller. This lowers the center of gravity and reduces the weight at the top, making the tower less likely to topple.

D. The 10-Second Rule

After every few levels, let go and count to 10. If it stands, keep going. If it falls, you've found your weak point — fix it before adding more.

Step 4 — How Tall Can You Go?

Measure your tower! Use a tape measure or ruler. The tower must stand on its own for a full 10 seconds to count.

Challenge Levels

Basic

Build a tower at least 12 inches tall that stands on its own. Focus on getting a solid, stable structure.

Medium

Build a tower at least 24 inches tall. You'll need diagonal bracing on every level to hit this height.

Advanced

Build a tower 36+ inches tall. At this height, every connection matters. Can your tower also survive a gentle breeze (someone waving a folder)?
