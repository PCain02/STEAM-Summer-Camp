The Scientific Method

Every great discovery starts with a question. Learn how real scientists investigate the world — then use the same process to run your own experiment from start to finish. You'll form a hypothesis, collect data, and draw conclusions just like a pro.

Think Like a Scientist

Scientists aren't just people in lab coats — they're curious people who ask questions and test answers. Doctors use the scientific method to figure out why someone is sick. Engineers use it to test if a new bridge design is safe. Farmers use it to learn which crops grow best. You'll use it throughout this entire camp!

Science Is Everywhere

When you wonder "Why is the sky blue?" or "Will my plant grow faster in sunlight?" — that's science starting! Every question can become an investigation.

Your Community Connection

You'll use the scientific method in every project this camp — testing bridges, growing plants, building circuits, and more. This is your foundation.

Work as a Team

Real scientists collaborate. Your community of 6 will share ideas, challenge each other's thinking, and build on each other's discoveries.

Step 1 — Observation

Every experiment begins by paying attention. Scientists observe the world around them and notice things that make them curious.

What Is an Observation?

An observation is something you notice using your five senses — what you see, hear, smell, touch, or taste. For example: "The plants near the window are taller than the ones in the corner." That's an observation! You aren't guessing why yet — just noticing what's happening.

Try It Now

Look around your room right now. Write down 3 observations — things you notice that make you curious. Don't explain them yet, just describe what you see. For example: "The ice in my water melted faster today than yesterday" or "My phone charges faster with one cable than another."

Step 2 — Ask a Question

Now turn your observation into a question you can actually test.

Good Questions vs. Bad Questions

A testable question is one you can answer by doing an experiment. "Why is the sky blue?" is interesting but hard to test in a classroom. "Does warm water freeze faster than cold water?" is something you can actually test! Good science questions usually start with "Does," "How," "What," or "Which."

Good: Testable

"Does the color of light affect how fast a plant grows?" — You can set this up and measure it!

Not Great: Too Vague

"Why do plants grow?" — This is way too broad. What part of growing? Under what conditions? Be specific!

Step 3 — Form a Hypothesis

A hypothesis is your educated guess — what you think will happen and why.

The "If... Then... Because..." Formula

The easiest way to write a hypothesis is: "If [I do this], then [this will happen], because [this reason]." For example: "If I water a plant with soda instead of water, then it will grow slower, because the sugar might block the roots from absorbing nutrients."

It's Okay to Be Wrong!

Here's a secret: scientists are wrong all the time. A hypothesis that turns out to be wrong is still useful — you learned something! The point isn't to guess right. The point is to test your guess fairly and learn from the results.

Step 4 — Design Your Experiment

This is where you actually test your hypothesis. But you need to be fair about it — that's where variables come in.

What Are Variables?

A variable is anything that can change in your experiment. There are three types:

Independent variable — the ONE thing you change on purpose (example: the amount of water).

Dependent variable — the thing you measure to see what happened (example: how tall the plant grows).

Controlled variables — everything you keep the SAME so your test is fair (example: same type of plant, same soil, same sunlight).

The Fair Test Rule

If you change more than one thing at a time, you won't know which change caused the result! That's why real scientists only change ONE variable at a time. Everything else stays exactly the same.

Pro Tip: Write It Down First

Before you start, write out your full plan: What materials do you need? What steps will you follow? What will you measure? How many times will you repeat it?

Pro Tip: Repeat It

Doing your experiment only once isn't enough. What if something weird happened that one time? Scientists repeat experiments at least 3 times to make sure the results are reliable.

Step 5 — Collect and Analyze Data

Data is the information you collect during your experiment. This is the evidence that supports (or disproves) your hypothesis.

Recording Data

Use a data table to keep your information organized. Label your columns clearly. Write down your measurements as you go — don't try to remember them later! Include the date, time, and any notes about things that seemed unusual.

Making Sense of It

Look for patterns in your data. Did the numbers go up? Down? Stay the same? Create a simple graph (bar graph or line graph) to visualize your results. Pictures make patterns way easier to spot than staring at a bunch of numbers.

Step 6 — Draw Your Conclusion

Time to answer the big question: Was your hypothesis right?

Writing a Conclusion

Your conclusion should answer these questions: What did you find out? Was your hypothesis supported or not? What's the evidence? Point to your specific data. What would you do differently? If you ran the experiment again, how could you improve it? What new questions do you have? Good science always leads to more questions!

Share Your Findings

Scientists share their work so others can learn from it and try to repeat it. Present your findings to your community. Explain what you did, what you found, and what you'd do next. Be ready for questions!

Your Experiment

Now it's your turn! Pick a question, follow all six steps, and run your own experiment. Here are some ideas to get you started:

Paper Airplane Distance

Does the type of paper affect how far a paper airplane flies? Test printer paper, construction paper, and newspaper.

Dissolving Speed

Does water temperature affect how fast sugar dissolves? Test cold, room temperature, and hot water.

Magnet Strength

Does the number of magnets stacked together change how many paperclips they can hold? Test 1, 2, and 3 magnets.

Challenge Levels

Choose the level that matches where you are — and try to push yourself one step further!

Basic

Follow the six steps with a guided experiment from the list above. Fill in a provided data table, create a simple bar graph, and write a 3-sentence conclusion. Your teacher will help you through each step.

Medium

Come up with your own testable question. Design the experiment from scratch — choose your variables, create your own data table, and make a graph. Write a full conclusion paragraph explaining your results.

Advanced

Design your own experiment AND present your findings as a scientific poster or report. Include a title, hypothesis, procedure, data table, graph, conclusion, and "future research" section. Present to the class and answer questions.
