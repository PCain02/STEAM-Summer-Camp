Environmental Science

How do plants survive — and what do they need to thrive? In this project you'll study how moisture and light affect plant growth by designing controlled experiments, collecting data over multiple days, and presenting your findings in a scientific poster and report.

Why Plants Matter

Plants aren't just decoration — they're the foundation of life on Earth. They produce the oxygen we breathe, provide food for every living creature, prevent soil from washing away, and cool down neighborhoods on hot days. Understanding what plants need helps us build healthier communities.

Green Spaces in Your Town

Parks, gardens, and tree-lined streets make communities cooler, cleaner, and happier. What green spaces exist in your neighborhood? What would you add?

Food Comes from Plants

Every meal you eat traces back to a plant. Even meat comes from animals that ate plants! Understanding plant growth = understanding where food comes from.

Climate and Plants

Plants absorb carbon dioxide — a gas that warms the planet. More plants = less CO2 in the air. Your experiment helps you understand how to help plants grow their best.

Step 1 — What Is Environmental Science?

Environmental science is the study of how living things interact with their surroundings. It combines biology, chemistry, and earth science to understand how the natural world works — and how humans affect it.

Your Focus: Plant Growth

Plants need four main things to grow: light (energy from the sun), water (moisture for cells), nutrients (food from the soil), and air (carbon dioxide for photosynthesis). Today you'll investigate what happens when you change the amount of light or water a plant receives.

What Is Photosynthesis?

Plants make their own food through photosynthesis. They take in light energy, water, and carbon dioxide, then convert them into sugar (food) and oxygen (which we breathe!). It's like a tiny factory inside every leaf. Change the inputs, and the factory works differently.

Step 2 — Understanding Variables

To run a fair experiment, you need to understand variables — the things that can change.

Independent Variable (What You Change)

This is the ONE thing you deliberately change. For your experiment, this might be the amount of water (1 tablespoon vs. 3 tablespoons vs. 5 tablespoons per day) OR the amount of light (full sun vs. shade vs. closet darkness).

Dependent Variable (What You Measure)

This is what you observe and measure to see the effect. In a plant experiment, you'd measure: plant height (in cm), number of leaves, leaf color (green = healthy, yellow = stressed), or overall health on a 1-5 scale.

Controlled Variables (What Stays the Same)

Everything EXCEPT your independent variable must stay identical. That means: same type of plant or seed, same type and amount of soil, same size pot, same temperature/location (except for light experiments), and same time of day for watering.

Why Control Matters

If you give one plant more water AND more sunlight, and it grows taller — was it the water or the sun? You can't tell! Only change ONE thing at a time so you know exactly what caused the result.

The Control Group

Always have one plant that gets "normal" conditions — regular water, regular light. This is your control group. You compare all other plants against this one to measure the difference.

Step 3 — Set Up Your Experiment

Time to get your hands dirty! Here's how to set up a controlled plant experiment.

Materials You'll Need

6-9 small pots or cups (with drainage holes poked in the bottom), potting soil, seeds (bean seeds or fast-growing grass seed work great), a ruler (for measuring in centimeters), a watering device (measuring spoons or a small cup), labels or tape and a marker, and your science notebook for recording data.

Option A: Testing Moisture

Plant 3 groups of seeds (at least 2 pots per group). Give each group a different amount of water daily: Group 1 gets 1 tablespoon (low water), Group 2 gets 3 tablespoons (medium water), Group 3 gets 5 tablespoons (high water). Keep ALL pots in the same light and temperature conditions.

Option B: Testing Light

Plant 3 groups of seeds. Place each group in a different light condition: Group 1 in direct sunlight (window sill), Group 2 in indirect light (middle of room), Group 3 in darkness (inside a closet or covered with a box). Give ALL pots the exact same amount of water daily.

Label Everything!

Label each pot clearly with the group name, date planted, and the specific condition (e.g., "Group 2 - Medium Water - 3 tbsp/day"). Trust us — after a few days, you WILL forget which pot is which if you don't label them.

Step 4 — Collect Data Over Time

Science takes patience. You'll observe and record data every day for several days.

Your Data Table

Create a table in your science notebook with these columns: Date, Day Number (Day 1, Day 2...), Group Name, Height (cm), Number of Leaves, Color/Health, and Notes (anything unusual you notice). Measure at the same time each day for consistency.

Be Precise

Measure from the soil surface to the tip of the tallest part of the plant. Use centimeters, not inches (scientists use metric!). If a seed hasn't sprouted yet, record "0 cm." Write down everything — even "nothing changed today" is useful data.

Take Photos

If possible, take a picture of your plants each day from the same angle. Visual records help you see changes that are hard to measure with a ruler.

Observe, Don't Just Measure

Numbers are important, but also note what you SEE. Are leaves drooping? Is the soil dry or soggy? Is there mold growing? These qualitative observations add richness to your data.

Step 5 — Analyze Your Results

Now that you have data, it's time to figure out what it means.

Make a Graph

Create a line graph with "Day Number" on the x-axis (bottom) and "Plant Height (cm)" on the y-axis (side). Plot a separate colored line for each group. This lets you visually compare how fast each group grew over time.

Look for Patterns

Which group grew the tallest? Which grew the fastest (steepest line)? Did any group stop growing or get shorter? Was there a "sweet spot" amount of water or light that worked best? Did any result surprise you?

Draw Your Conclusion

Write a conclusion paragraph that answers: Was your hypothesis supported by the data? What specific evidence (numbers!) supports your answer? What do your results tell us about what plants need? What questions do you still have?

Step 6 — Create Your Poster and Report

Real scientists present their findings so others can learn from them. You'll create a scientific poster and a written report.

Your Scientific Poster

Your poster should include these sections: Title (catchy but descriptive), Question (what were you investigating?), Hypothesis (what did you predict?), Materials and Method (what did you do?), Data (your table and graph), Results (what happened?), Conclusion (what did you learn?), and Photos of your plants if you have them.

Your Written Report

Write a 1-page report that tells the story of your experiment from start to finish. Use paragraphs, include your data, and explain what you found out. End with what you would investigate next if you had more time.

Poster Design Tips

Use big, clear headings. Put your graph front and center — it's the star of the show. Use color to make it eye-catching. Make sure someone can understand your experiment just by reading the poster.

Present Like a Scientist

Stand by your poster and explain it to visitors. Start with your question, walk them through your method, show them the data, and end with your conclusion. Be ready for "Why?" questions!

Challenge Levels

Choose the level that fits your skills — then try to stretch to the next one!

Basic

Follow the guided experiment (Option A or B) with your community group. Use the provided data table template to record your observations each day. Create a bar graph comparing final plant heights. Write a 3-sentence conclusion and contribute to a group poster.

Medium

Complete the guided experiment, then test a second variable. If you tested water first, now test light (or vice versa). Design your own data table. Create a line graph showing growth over time. Write a full conclusion paragraph and create your own section of the poster.

Advanced

Design your own original plant experiment (different soil types? music? temperature?). Calculate the average growth rate per day for each group. Discuss possible sources of error in your experiment. Write a full scientific report with all sections, and present your findings with statistical evidence to the class.
